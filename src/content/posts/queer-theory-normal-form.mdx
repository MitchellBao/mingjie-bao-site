One way to read queer theory is as a critique of normal form. A society does not only punish deviance after the fact. It also produces templates for legible bodies, lives, desires, relationships, and futures.

The point is not simply to celebrate difference as an aesthetic. The more difficult question is how norms become invisible infrastructure: what counts as maturity, health, intimacy, success, danger, consent, privacy, or public order.

## Autonomy and Legibility

Autonomy is not only the freedom to choose among recognized options. It also includes the ability to resist being reduced to the options that institutions already know how to process.

This makes queer critique relevant beyond identity categories. It becomes a way to examine how power asks people to become administratively readable before they are allowed to be socially real.
