Security systems sometimes depend on a gap between what humans can perceive and what machines can classify. CAPTCHAs are the obvious example, but the broader pattern appears in content moderation, biometric authentication, watermarking, and adversarial examples.

The phrase "human-machine perception mismatch" is useful because it avoids treating either side as a fixed standard. Human perception is variable, situated, trained, fatigued, and embodied. Machine perception is also plural: a narrow ASR model, a multimodal foundation model, and a specialized classifier may fail in different ways.

## Why This Matters

When a system assumes that noise is hard for machines but tolerable for humans, the assumption should be revisited whenever model capabilities change. Large audio-language models may not perceive sound like humans, but they may still become good enough at the task that the old boundary stops being protective.

The practical question is not whether machines "hear like us." It is whether their errors remain different enough to preserve the security property.
