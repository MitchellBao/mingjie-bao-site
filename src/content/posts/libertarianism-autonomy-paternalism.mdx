The strongest version of libertarian political thought is not a mood of refusal. It is an argument about moral authority: who may decide for whom, under what conditions, and with what burden of justification.

Paternalism becomes difficult because it often begins with a plausible concern. People can be misinformed, pressured, addicted, manipulated, or trapped inside bad options. The philosophical problem is not whether these conditions exist. The problem is whether coercive correction by a state or collective authority actually respects the agency it claims to protect.

## A Working Distinction

An anti-paternalist position does not need to deny vulnerability. It can instead ask whether institutions that claim protective authority are themselves accountable, epistemically modest, and constrained.

This is where libertarianism, feminism, and critiques of moral paternalism can become more interesting than their caricatures. The shared question is how autonomy survives in conditions where social power is real but centralized correction may create new forms of domination.
