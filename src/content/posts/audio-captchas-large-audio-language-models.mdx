Audio CAPTCHAs historically rely on an asymmetry between human auditory perception and automated speech recognition. A challenge can be noisy, distorted, segmented, or semantically awkward, while still remaining usable for many human listeners.

Large audio-language models complicate that assumption. They can combine transcription, acoustic scene understanding, instruction following, and lightweight reasoning in a single interface. This makes the relevant attack surface broader than the classic question of whether an ASR system can produce the right transcript.

## Working Hypothesis

The security of an audio CAPTCHA should be evaluated as an end-to-end task. If a model can listen to the audio, infer the likely challenge format, reason over uncertainty, and output the expected answer, then transcription accuracy alone is not enough to describe the risk.

## Questions

- How much does zero-shot prompting improve attack success?
- Which distortions still separate human recognition from model recognition?
- Do models exploit regularities in challenge generation?
- How should evaluation account for accessibility and usability constraints?
